# Local SEO — Senior Consultant's Playbook

## The Local Algorithm

Google's local results (the "Local Pack" / "Map Pack") use three primary factors:

1. **Relevance:** How well your business matches the search query. Driven by categories, attributes, keywords in your profile and website.
2. **Distance:** How close your business is to the searcher (or the location specified in the query). You can't control this.
3. **Prominence:** How well-known/authoritative your business is. Driven by reviews, citations, links, brand mentions, and overall SEO.

**Key insight:** Distance is the strongest factor for most local queries. A mediocre business 0.5 miles away will often outrank an excellent business 5 miles away. This means you need to focus optimization on the factors you CAN control: relevance and prominence.

## Google Business Profile (GBP) Optimization

### Primary Information

- **Business name:** EXACTLY as it appears in the real world. Don't keyword stuff. "Joe's Plumbing" not "Joe's Plumbing | Best Plumber in Austin | 24/7 Emergency Plumbing." Google will suspend you for this.
- **Primary category:** The most important ranking signal you control. Choose the most specific category that matches your core service. "Personal Injury Attorney" not "Lawyer."
- **Secondary categories:** Add ALL relevant categories (up to 9 additional). These trigger visibility for related searches.
- **Address:** Exact match across all citations. If your address is "123 Main St, Suite 200" use that exact format everywhere.
- **Service area:** For service-area businesses (plumbers, cleaners, etc.), set service areas instead of showing an address. Don't set an unrealistically large area.
- **Phone number:** Use a local number (not 800/toll-free) as your primary. Local numbers are a relevance signal.
- **Website:** Link to your homepage or a location-specific landing page (for multi-location businesses).
- **Hours:** Keep accurate. Update for holidays. Google shows "Open now" as a filter — wrong hours = lost customers.

### Content Optimization

- **Business description:** 750 characters max. Include primary keywords naturally. Describe what makes you unique. Don't stuff keywords.
- **Services/Products:** Add every service/product with descriptions. These are indexed and help relevance matching.
- **Attributes:** Complete every relevant attribute (wheelchair accessible, free Wi-Fi, women-owned, etc.). These appear in your listing and can be filter criteria.

### Visual Content

- **Photos:** Businesses with 100+ photos get 520% more calls than average (BrightLocal data). Upload:
  - Exterior (helps Google match your location)
  - Interior
  - Team/staff
  - Products/services in action
  - Before/after (for home services, dental, etc.)
- **Cover photo:** Best image that represents your business
- **Logo:** Upload a clean, recognizable logo
- **Videos:** Up to 30-second videos. Show your business, services, team.
- **Update regularly:** Upload new photos weekly/monthly. Fresh visual content signals an active business.

### Google Posts

- Post weekly at minimum. Types: Updates, Events, Offers, Products
- Include a photo, 150-300 words, and a CTA button (Book, Learn more, Call, etc.)
- Posts expire after 7 days (except Events which last until the event date)
- Use keywords naturally in posts
- Include UTM parameters on links for tracking

### Q&A Section

- **Pre-seed your own Q&A.** Ask and answer common questions about your business (using your personal Google account, not your business account — Google allows this).
- Monitor for new questions — unanswered questions look bad
- Upvote the most helpful Q&As to keep them prominent
- Competitors can post misleading questions — monitor and report if needed

## NAP Consistency

**NAP = Name, Address, Phone Number.** This must be IDENTICAL everywhere your business appears online.

### Why It Matters

Google cross-references your NAP across the web to verify your business information. Inconsistencies create confusion and dilute your local signals. Even small differences matter:
- "St" vs "Street" vs "St."
- "Suite 200" vs "Ste 200" vs "#200"
- "(512) 555-1234" vs "512-555-1234"

**Pick one canonical format and use it everywhere.**

### Key Citation Sources (US)

#### Tier 1 — Must Have (data aggregators that feed hundreds of smaller directories)
1. **Data Axle (InfoUSA)** — feeds YP.com, SuperPages, and hundreds of local directories
2. **Localeze (Neustar)** — feeds major search engines and apps
3. **Foursquare** — feeds Apple Maps, Uber, Snapchat, and thousands of apps
4. **Factual (now part of Foursquare)** — same network

**Action:** Submit accurate NAP to all four aggregators. This cascades to hundreds of downstream directories. Use a service like BrightLocal, Yext, or Whitespark to manage this.

#### Tier 2 — Major Platforms
- Google Business Profile
- Apple Maps (Apple Business Connect)
- Bing Places
- Facebook Business Page
- Yelp
- BBB (Better Business Bureau)
- Yellow Pages / YP.com
- Nextdoor (for neighborhood businesses)

#### Tier 3 — Industry-Specific (examples)
- **Restaurants:** TripAdvisor, OpenTable, DoorDash, Uber Eats, Grubhub
- **Medical:** Healthgrades, Vitals, WebMD, Zocdoc, RealSelf
- **Legal:** Avvo, FindLaw, Justia, Lawyers.com, Super Lawyers
- **Home services:** Angi (Angie's List), HomeAdvisor, Thumbtack, Houzz
- **Real estate:** Zillow, Realtor.com, Trulia
- **Automotive:** Cars.com, AutoTrader, CarGurus, DealerRater

**Find your industry's directories:** Search "[industry] directories" or check where your competitors are listed (Whitespark's Local Citation Finder is excellent for this).

## Review Management

### Why Reviews Are Critical

- Reviews are the #3 local ranking factor (after GBP signals and on-page SEO)
- Star rating affects CTR dramatically (4.0+ is the threshold for most consumers)
- Review quantity signals popularity and trustworthiness
- Review keywords help relevance (when customers mention services naturally)
- Review recency matters — a business with 500 reviews but nothing in 6 months looks stale

### Review Generation Strategy

1. **Ask at the point of maximum satisfaction.** Right after delivering results, completing a service, or getting a compliment.
2. **Make it easy.** Create a short URL for your Google review page:
   - GBP dashboard → "Get more reviews" → copy the short link
   - Or create: `https://search.google.com/local/writereview?placeid=YOUR_PLACE_ID`
3. **Multi-channel asking:**
   - In-person request (highest conversion)
   - SMS after service (second highest)
   - Email follow-up with direct link
   - QR code on receipts, business cards, signage
   - Post-service automated email/SMS sequence
4. **Volume target:** Aim for consistent velocity. 4-8 new reviews/month is better than 50 in one month then nothing for 6 months.
5. **Don't incentivize reviews.** No "leave a review for 10% off." Violates Google's policies and can get reviews removed.
6. **Don't gate reviews.** Asking "was your experience positive?" and only sending happy customers to Google is against policy.

### Review Response

**Respond to every review.** Every single one.

**Positive review response template:**
```
Thank you [Name]! We're thrilled that [reference specific detail from their review]. 
[Your team member/aspect of service] really appreciates the kind words. 
Looking forward to helping you with [related service] in the future!
```

**Negative review response template:**
```
[Name], thank you for sharing your feedback. We're sorry to hear about your experience 
with [specific issue]. This isn't the standard we hold ourselves to. We'd like to make 
this right — please contact [Name] at [phone/email] so we can address this directly.
```

**Rules:**
- Respond within 24-48 hours (faster is better)
- Never argue publicly. Take it offline.
- Be specific — reference details from their review to show you actually read it
- Include keywords naturally (Google indexes review responses)
- Keep it human — no corporate boilerplate
- For fake/spam reviews: respond professionally, then flag for removal via GBP

### Multi-Location Schema

For businesses with multiple locations, each location page should have its own LocalBusiness schema with unique NAP. The organization schema on the homepage should reference all locations:

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Organization",
  "name": "Joe's Plumbing",
  "url": "https://joesplumbing.com",
  "subOrganization": [
    {
      "@type": "LocalBusiness",
      "name": "Joe's Plumbing - Austin",
      "url": "https://joesplumbing.com/austin/"
    },
    {
      "@type": "LocalBusiness",
      "name": "Joe's Plumbing - San Antonio",
      "url": "https://joesplumbing.com/san-antonio/"
    }
  ]
}
```

## Local Keyword Research

### Keyword Patterns

| Pattern | Example | Volume Level |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| `[service] [city]` | "plumber austin" | Highest |
| `[service] near me` | "plumber near me" | High (Google localizes) |
| `[service] in [neighborhood]` | "plumber in south austin" | Medium |
| `best [service] [city]` | "best plumber austin" | Medium-High |
| `[service] [city] [qualifier]` | "emergency plumber austin" | Medium |
| `[service] [zip code]` | "plumber 78701" | Low |
| `[service] open now` | "plumber open now" | Medium (mobile) |

### Local Keyword Strategy

1. **Build a core keyword list:** Your services × your target locations
2. **Don't forget "near me" variants.** Google automatically localizes these, but having "near me" pages can help for broader reach.
3. **Neighborhood-level keywords** can be less competitive and more targeted. Create location-specific content for areas you serve.
4. **Service-specific landing pages:** Don't try to rank one page for "plumber," "water heater repair," "drain cleaning," and "pipe repair." Create dedicated pages for each service in each major location.

## Location Pages (Multi-Location Businesses)

### Good Location Page Structure

```
URL: /locations/austin-tx/

H1: Plumbing Services in Austin, TX
- Intro paragraph (unique to this location, mention local landmarks, neighborhoods served)
- Services offered at this location (link to service-specific pages)
- LocalBusiness schema with this location's NAP
- Embedded Google Map
- Location-specific reviews/testimonials
- Team members at this location (with photos)
- Directions/parking info
- Hours of operation
- CTA: Call/book for this location
- NAP in footer consistent with GBP listing
```

### What NOT to Do

- **Doorway pages:** Creating 50 nearly identical pages for every city variation ("plumber dallas," "plumber fort worth," "plumber arlington") with just the city name swapped. Google's Doorway Pages algorithm penalizes this.
- **Fake locations:** Listing addresses where you don't have a physical presence. GBP will suspend you.
- **Thin location pages:** A page with just "We serve [City]. Call us!" adds no value.

**Each location page must have genuinely unique, valuable content.** Mention local neighborhoods, specific projects in that area, team members who serve that region, local partnerships, and community involvement.

## Local Link Building

### High-Value Local Link Sources

1. **Chamber of Commerce** — Membership usually includes a directory listing with a link
2. **Local business associations** — Industry groups, BNI chapters, etc.
3. **Local news/media** — Sponsor local events, submit press releases for genuinely newsworthy events, offer expert commentary on local issues
4. **Local charities/nonprofits** — Sponsor or volunteer, get listed as a partner/sponsor
5. **Local universities/colleges** — Scholarship programs, guest lectures, internship programs
6. **Local bloggers/influencers** — Collaborate on content, host events
7. **Complementary businesses** — A plumber links to their preferred HVAC company and vice versa (natural, relevant cross-promotion)
8. **Local event sponsorship** — Community events, sports teams, festivals
9. **Government/city websites** — Some have business directories or community resource pages

### Local Digital PR

- **Data about your city/region.** "Average cost of [service] in [city] in 2025" type content
- **Community involvement stories** that local media would cover
- **Expert commentary** on local issues related to your industry (e.g., a plumber commenting on a city water quality report)

## Proximity & the Local Algorithm

You cannot change proximity. But you can influence it:

1. **Verify your exact address** with a pin in Google Maps. If the pin is in the wrong location, move it in GBP.
2. **Service area businesses:** Your ranking center is your listed address. Locate strategically.
3. **Multi-location businesses:** Each location creates a ranking center. If you serve a large metro area, consider multiple offices.
4. **For queries without a location modifier** ("plumber" vs "plumber austin"), Google uses the searcher's exact location heavily. You'll rank better for searches within 5 miles of your address than 15 miles.
5. **Build hyper-local signals:** Content mentioning specific neighborhoods, landmarks, and local events. Links from local organizations. Citations in local directories.

## Tracking Local Rankings

Local rankings vary by exact searcher location, making tracking tricky.

### Tools
- **BrightLocal:** Grid-based local rank tracking (shows rankings from multiple points across a map)
- **Local Falcon:** Similar grid-based tracking with visual heat maps
- **Whitespark:** Local rank tracker + citation management
- **GBP Insights:** Direct data on how customers find your listing (searches, views, actions)

### Key Metrics to Track

| Metric | Source | Why It Matters |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| GBP Profile Views | GBP Insights | Overall visibility |
| GBP Actions (calls, directions, website clicks) | GBP Insights | Conversion activity |
| Search queries triggering your listing | GBP Insights | Keyword relevance |
| Local Pack appearances | Rank tracker | Visibility for target keywords |
| Organic traffic to location pages | GA4 | Website-based local traffic |
| Review count & rating | GBP | Social proof + ranking factor |
| Citation accuracy score | BrightLocal/Whitespark | NAP consistency |
| Local keyword rankings | Rank tracker | Competitive position |
