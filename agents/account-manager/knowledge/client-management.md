# Client Management — Expert Knowledge Base

## Onboarding Process

### Pre-Kickoff (Before First Meeting)
1. **Sales handoff document** — review everything the sales team promised: scope, deliverables, pricing, timeline, client expectations, pain points, why they signed
2. **Account setup checklist**:
   - Platform access requests (Meta Business Manager, Google Ads, TikTok, GA4, Shopify)
   - Brand assets collection (logos, fonts, colors, brand guidelines)
   - Content library request (existing photos, videos, UGC)
   - Competitor list from client
   - Historical performance data (last 3-6 months minimum)
3. **Internal team briefing** — brief the editors, media buyers, and strategists on the new client before kickoff
4. **Research** — spend 30 minutes reviewing their website, social profiles, competitors, and industry. Come to kickoff informed.

### Kickoff Meeting Structure (60-90 Minutes)
**Agenda:**
1. **Introductions** (5 min) — who's on the team, roles, who the client contacts for what
2. **Client's business overview** (15 min) — their story, product/service, target customer, competitive landscape. *Let them talk.* You'll learn things that weren't in the sales handoff.
3. **Goals & KPIs** (15 min) — what does success look like in 30/60/90 days? Get specific numbers: revenue targets, lead volume, ROAS targets, growth goals.
4. **Current state review** (15 min) — what's working, what's not, what they've tried before, why they left their last agency (if applicable — this reveals their pain points)
5. **Our process** (10 min) — how we work, communication cadence, tools we use, what we need from them, approval workflows
6. **Content & creative discussion** (15 min) — brand voice, content preferences, what they love/hate, any sensitivities or no-go topics
7. **Next steps & timeline** (10 min) — first deliverables, deadlines, next meeting date

**After kickoff:**
- Send recap email within 24 hours: key decisions, action items with owners, timeline
- Create Monday.com board for the client
- Schedule recurring weekly check-in
- Set up shared Slack/communication channel

### First 30 Days — Setting the Foundation
- **Week 1**: Complete account audits, competitive analysis, strategy document draft
- **Week 2**: Present strategy for approval, begin content production, launch initial campaigns
- **Week 3**: First performance check-in, initial optimizations, content calendar review
- **Week 4**: First monthly report, 30-day review meeting, adjust strategy based on data

**Critical**: Over-communicate in the first 30 days. This is when trust is built or lost. Send updates even when there's nothing major to report — silence makes clients nervous.

## Communication Cadence

### Weekly Touchpoints
- **Weekly check-in call** (30 min): Performance update, what's working/not, upcoming content, any blockers
- **Async Slack/email updates** (2-3× per week): Quick wins, content approvals, questions
- **Content approvals**: Send batched (not one-at-a-time) with clear deadlines for feedback

### Monthly Touchpoints
- **Monthly performance report** (see reporting-templates.md): Comprehensive data review
- **Monthly strategy call** (45-60 min): Deep dive into performance, strategic adjustments, next month's plan
- **Content calendar review**: Next month's planned content for approval

### Quarterly Touchpoints
- **Quarterly Business Review (QBR)** (60-90 min): Big-picture performance vs goals, strategic roadmap, market trends, scope review
- **Scope/pricing review** — if applicable, adjust scope based on performance and client needs

### Communication Rules
1. **Respond within 4 business hours** to client messages (even if just to acknowledge and say you'll follow up)
2. **Never surprise the client** — if something is going wrong (campaign underperforming, deadline slipping), tell them proactively before they notice
3. **Lead with solutions** — don't just report problems: "X happened, here's what we're doing about it, and here's the expected outcome"
4. **Manage up** — tell clients what you need from them and when: "We need creative approval by Thursday to stay on schedule for Monday's launch"
5. **Document everything** — important decisions, scope changes, approvals should be in writing (email or shared doc, not just verbal)

## Setting and Managing Expectations

### Setting Expectations (Onboarding)
- **Timeline expectations**: "Months 1-2 are learning and testing. You'll see meaningful traction in month 3. Full optimization by month 4-6."
- **Performance expectations**: Give ranges, not guarantees. "Based on your industry and budget, we typically see CPAs of $20-40 in the first 90 days."
- **Creative expectations**: "We'll need to test 10-15 creatives before finding consistent winners. Not every piece of content will be a hit — that's the process."
- **Budget expectations**: "Your budget supports X campaigns across Y platforms. Adding Z would require additional investment."
- **Approval expectations**: "We need feedback within 48 hours to maintain our content calendar. Delays in approvals = delays in posting."

### Managing Expectations (Ongoing)
- **When performance dips**: Explain why (seasonality, creative fatigue, algorithm changes, iOS updates) and what you're doing about it. Context prevents panic.
- **When they compare to competitors**: "Competitor X may have 10× your budget, different margins, or different goals. Let's focus on your benchmarks, not theirs."
- **When they want more for less**: Be honest about trade-offs: "We can add TikTok, but something else will get less attention. Here's what I'd recommend reprioritizing."
- **When they want instant results**: "Paid media isn't a light switch. The algorithm needs data, testing takes time, and creative development has a production cycle. Here's our 90-day roadmap."

## Handling Difficult Conversations

### Performance Underperformance
**Framework**: Acknowledge → Explain → Action Plan → Timeline
> "I want to be transparent — our results this month were below target. [Specific metrics]. Here's what happened: [explanation without excuses]. Here's our plan to fix it: [3 specific actions]. We expect to see improvement within [timeline]. I'll update you [frequency]."

### Scope Creep
**Signs**: Client asking for "quick" extras, "can you also…", "while you're at it…"
**Response framework**:
> "Great idea — I think that could be really valuable. That falls outside our current scope, so let me put together what it would look like to add that. I'll have a proposal for you by [date]."

Never say "that's not in the scope" without offering a path forward. But don't do free work — it devalues your services and sets a precedent.

### Client Wants to Micromanage
> "I appreciate how invested you are in the details — it shows how much you care about this. I want to make sure we're using your time wisely. How about we [proposed cadence/process] so you have visibility without needing to be in every decision? That frees you up for [their actual job] while we handle the execution."

### Client Is Unresponsive
**Escalation path:**
1. Follow up via primary channel (Slack/email) with specific deadline
2. Follow up via secondary channel (text/call) 48 hours later
3. Send "blocker" email: "We're blocked on [deliverable] until we get [what you need]. Without this by [date], [consequence: launch delays, missed window, etc.]"
4. If still no response: escalate to their manager/partner/decision-maker

### Delivering Bad News
- **Don't bury it** — lead with the issue, then context, then solution
- **Don't blame the client** — even if they caused it (late approvals, budget cuts), frame it collaboratively
- **Be specific** — "Performance dropped 20%" not "things are a bit soft"
- **Always pair with a plan** — never deliver bad news without next steps

## Escalation Procedures

### Internal Escalation
| Situation | Escalate To | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Client unhappy with deliverable quality | Creative Director / Team Lead | Same day |
| Campaign significantly underperforming (>30% off target for 2+ weeks) | Media Director / Strategist | Within 48 hours |
| Client threatens to leave | Account Director / Owner | Immediately |
| Scope disagreement | Account Director | Within 24 hours |
| Team capacity/deadline issues | Project Manager / Ops Lead | As soon as identified |
| Legal/compliance concern | Owner / Legal | Immediately |

### Client Escalation
When the client escalates past you:
1. **Don't take it personally** — it's about the business, not you
2. **Brief your leadership** immediately with context, what happened, and your recommended solution
3. **Join the escalation call** — you know the account best
4. **Document the resolution** and follow up with the client to rebuild trust
5. **Post-mortem internally** — what caused the escalation, how to prevent it

## Retention Strategies

### Proactive Retention (Preventing Churn Before It Happens)
1. **Deliver consistent wins** — share wins weekly, even small ones. Clients who feel like they're winning don't leave.
2. **Be indispensable** — know their business better than they expect. Reference their goals, their customers, their competitors in conversations.
3. **Anticipate needs** — "I noticed your competitors launched a new product line. Want us to adjust our strategy?"
4. **QBRs that show ROI** — quarterly, show the total value you've delivered vs what they're paying. Make the ROI undeniable.
5. **Relationship depth** — know multiple stakeholders, not just one contact. If your champion leaves, you should have relationships with others.

### Churn Signals (Watch For)
- Slower response times to messages
- Declining meeting attendance
- Questioning costs or ROI more frequently
- Asking for "more deliverables" without wanting to increase budget
- Comparing you to other agencies
- New marketing hire (who may want "their own" agency)
- Skipping QBR or monthly review

### When a Client Wants to Leave
1. **Listen first** — understand the real reason (often not what they initially say)
2. **Don't get defensive** — acknowledge their concerns genuinely
3. **Present a save plan** — specific changes, timeline, and what success looks like
4. **Offer a trial period** — "Give us 60 days with this new approach. If you're not seeing improvement, we'll part ways with no hard feelings."
5. **If they're leaving no matter what** — be gracious, do a thorough transition, and ask for a referral or testimonial for the work you did do well. Many "lost" clients come back 6-12 months later.
